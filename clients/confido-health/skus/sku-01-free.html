§ The read

Six dimensions, scored from outside the wall.

Motion clarity

Yellow

Your product is sharp — three named agents (Sara · Ryan · Lilly) is a clear, repeatable taxonomy. But the buyer is fuzzy: hospital + specialty + FQHC + dental in the customer wall, with one positioning sentence trying to land for all four. ICP is spread across four motions, not one.

→ The "anti-ICP" — who you've stopped selling to in the last 90 days — is the test. We'd ask on the call.

Pipeline health

Green (likely)

$10M Series A in Sep 2025 means investors saw conversion data and pipeline coverage they liked. The "1M+ patients enabled" line tells us volume is real; the named-customer wall (York Hospital, Dallas Renal, Good Shepherd, Alpha Dental) tells us deals close.

→ The unknown: stage-by-stage conversion rates by buyer subtype. Volume hides variance.

Forecast discipline

Yellow (estimate)

Common Series A pattern: forecast accuracy is +/- 30% because the founder is still re-grading deals weekly by gut. Two parallel motions (enterprise hospital cycles vs. SMB dental closes) make the forecast harder, not easier — different time-in-stage, different commit math.

→ Sign of green: a CRM stage map that matches what AEs would tell you verbally.

Buyer access

Yellow

Strong individual references (York Hospital, Dallas Renal). Weaker pattern: across four buyer subtypes, the warm-intro network can't be deep on all four simultaneously. Most Series A AI healthtechs we see have one buyer subtype where they have real depth and three where they're cold-pitching.

→ The test: for the next 10 target accounts, can you get warm-intro'd to the named decision-maker?

Team execution

Red (probable)

$10M-raised Series A AI healthtech with a customer wall like yours = the founder is on every late-stage call. Across four buyer subtypes, with two parallel motions (enterprise + SMB), the founder can't scale this. The risk isn't quality — it's the rate at which AEs become founder-equivalent.

→ The fix: a documented founder deal-pattern doc + AE 30/60/90 with pass/fail milestones.

Tooling & ops

Green

11+ EHR integrations is category-leading at Series A. SOC 2 + ISO 27001 + HIPAA from day one is past-the-bar — most competitors stall here. Internal RevOps almost certainly lighter than the product RevTech, but tooling is not your bottleneck.

→ The risk: AI-native sales workflows (research, draft, summarize) are probably *under*-deployed inside a company that's selling AI to others.

§ What we did NOT do

The 30-min read is the sketch. The 100-pt diagnostic is the photograph.

To turn this estimate into a defensible number — the kind you can take to the board, or to the next executive hire — the engagement does what we couldn't from the outside.

  • Five recorded call audits against Bayshore's 12-criterion rubric. Per call: did the AE establish budget, map the buying committee, ask about the no-decision path, set explicit next steps.
  • CRM scan of the last 12 months. Stage-conversion benchmark by segment. Average deal size by buyer subtype. Win/loss by buyer type. Time-in-stage outliers.
  • 3–5 win/loss interviews — 2 wins, 2 losses, 1 stalled. Standard question set. The buyer's words, not the AE's interpretation.
  • The full 100-question scorecard applied. 30 questions you've already seen on our site; 70 the partner can only score after the audit.
  • Cohort benchmarks — where you sit vs. anonymized peer healthtechs at the same stage and category.
  • The prioritized fix roadmap — top 5 fixes ranked by impact × ease, each with a one-page brief and a "Bayshore would" or "you can run this internally" recommendation.
