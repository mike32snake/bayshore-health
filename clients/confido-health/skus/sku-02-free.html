§ The category, mapped

Three clusters. Where you sit, where the white space is.

Voice AI in healthcare is converging into three distinct positioning clusters. The vendors inside each cluster compete with each other; vendors across clusters compete on whether the buyer needs admin automation or clinical efficiency or broad workflow consolidation. Where you sit determines who you actually beat — and lose to.

— Cluster A —

Clinical documentation & ambient scribing

Abridge · Suki AI

Optimizes clinician productivity, documentation, coding accuracy. Voice is secondary (capture only). Buyers: enterprise health systems, AMCs. Confido does not directly compete here — but the "clinical AI = serious AI" narrative threatens admin-only positioning.

— Cluster B (where Confido sits) —

Administrative call-center automation

Hyro · Confido · Infinitus · Assort · Hello Patient · EliseAI · SuperDial

Optimizes high-volume admin calls, staff time savings, call deflection. Voice is the primary interface. Confido's distinctive posture: mid-market + specialty/dental focus. Hyro fights for health systems; Infinitus owns payors. Confido owns the rest.

— Cluster C —

Broad workflow automation (AI + RPA)

Notable · Memora (Commure)

Multiple back-office + patient-facing workflows. Voice is one component of a broader suite. Buyers: enterprise health systems, plans. Threat to Confido = bundling argument ("why buy 3 vendors when Notable does it all?").

→ Where the open ground is

Mid-market specialty practices. Hyro chases health systems. Infinitus chases payors. Notable bundles for enterprise. The 5–30-doc specialty group, the FQHC, the dental DSO — these are the buyers Confido's customer wall is already proving. And almost no one else is fighting hard for them. The 90-day plan would lean into this aggressively: anti-ICP everything else for 12 months, dominate the segment, then expand.

§ Three positioning gaps you own

Confido's defensible ground today.

Based on your three named agents (Sara · Ryan · Lilly), your customer mix, your $10M Series A coverage, and how the rest of Cluster B is positioning, three places you can credibly plant a flag that competitors can't easily contest in the next 12 months.

— Gap 01 —

Vertical credibility in mid-market and specialty

Dallas Renal Group + Alpha Dental + Good Shepherd FQHC + York Hospital is a customer mix none of your direct competitors can match. Hyro has 45+ enterprise health systems but no specialty/dental references. Infinitus is payor-focused. The "no dedicated staff training, sub-30-day implementation" line is exactly what mid-market specialty buyers need to hear.

How to lean in: publish 3–5 specialty case studies with hard numbers; build a vertical-specific landing page per buyer subtype.

— Gap 02 —

Patient engagement past scheduling — Ryan + Lilly

Most of Cluster B is one-trick: Hyro = scheduling, Assort = scheduling, Hello Patient = scheduling, SuperDial = payor calls. Confido's three-agent model explicitly extends to pre/post-op (Ryan) and care gaps + SDOH (Lilly). That positions you adjacent to clinical outcomes, not just admin cost-cutting. Your "3x follow-up encounters" metric quantifies it.

How to lean in: stop describing Confido as "voice AI for healthcare admin." Start describing it as "voice AI agents across the patient journey." Different category, less crowded.

— Gap 03 —

Reachability across care modalities (FQHC, dental, integrated)

Most competitors position deeply for one buyer type. Confido's diversified customer wall makes the "one platform for IDN-owned dental + FQHC + medical group" argument credible — particularly for health systems that own all three modalities and want to consolidate vendors. This is a Series B narrative.

How to lean in: get one IDN reference that uses Confido across 2+ modalities (e.g., their owned dental clinic + their FQHC affiliate). Sells the platform argument far better than four separate logos.

§ Three positioning risks you don't have an answer to today

What competitors are saying that you can't yet match.

— Risk 01 —

Clinical-grade credibility (Abridge, Suki)

Abridge: "Best in KLAS for Ambient AI · Forbes AI 50 · TIME Best Inventions." Suki: deep Epic / Oracle Health / athena / MEDITECH integrations + 14+ language clinical-grade speech. As clinical AI and admin AI converge in buyers' minds, Abridge's clinical validation makes admin-only voice AI feel "lighter weight." Confido has no equivalent clinical narrative.

What to do: publish a "clinical safety + accuracy" page with the receipts (SOC 2 Type 2, ISO 27001, real measurement data, false-positive escalation rates). Doesn't need to be clinical AI — just needs to clear the bar visibly.

— Risk 02 —

Payor dominance bleeding into provider sales (Infinitus)

Infinitus: "44% of Fortune 50" + named partnerships with CVS, Humana, UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, Cigna, Express Scripts. As payors deploy voice AI internally, they'll start pushing providers toward payor-integrated solutions. Infinitus is already positioned to bundle payor-side + provider-side voice into one suite.

What to do: answer the "why buy a provider-only voice AI when our payor uses Infinitus?" objection before you hear it. Either (a) publish payor-data integration commitments, or (b) lean hard into "we work for the provider's interests, not the payor's" — different value prop, defensible position.

— Risk 03 —

No-code / low-code commoditization (Notable Flow AI, Hyro)

Notable launched Flow AI in Q4 2025 — embedded low-code conversational assistant. Hyro markets a no-code platform with pre-built healthcare flows. The pitch: "build your own voice flows, no vendor lock-in." Your three-agent model (Sara/Ryan/Lilly) is opinionated but not technically defensible against in-house builds.

What to do: reframe the agent model as "ready out-of-the-box for the workflows that matter most" + lean into the "no IT team needed" angle (mid-market specialty doesn't have IT teams to build flows). Commoditization is a slow threat — but Series B fundraising will require an answer.

§ Two CMS tailwinds for 2026

Specific regulation, specific dates, specific dollars.

→ Effective Jan 1, 2026

CPT-2026 AI-Augmented Code Framework

The AMA introduced new "AI-Augmented" CPT codes recognizing services where AI assists physician judgment. Remote monitoring codes were lowered from 20 to 10 minute monthly minimums — enabling billing for the kind of automated patient check-ins and outbound engagement Confido's Lilly agent already does.

Why it matters for Confido: Lilly (care gaps, transitions of care, SDOH outreach) just became a billable service line. The conversation with a CMIO changes from "this saves staff time" to "this generates new code-able revenue." That's a different kind of meeting.

→ Effective 2026 (CMS guidance issued 2025)

Medicare Advantage AI Oversight Rule

CMS clarified that MA organizations cannot use AI to deny or terminate coverage without individual case review. Plans now need verification + explanation workflows in front of every coverage decision — exactly what voice AI patient-facing platforms are built for.

Why it matters for Confido: opens a payor-channel angle. Plans need verification/explanation tooling to stay compliant; Confido's voice agents (handling benefit verification, appointment confirmation, prior-auth follow-up) become a compliance enabler, not just an admin tool. New buyer, new budget line.

§ The one number that matters most

Healthcare voice AI raised $1.8B in Q1 2026 — and only 8% of providers' AI pilots reach production.

The category has money, momentum, and CMS tailwinds. But healthcare's production-deployment rate is the lowest of any AI sector — 8%. Why? Integration friction, compliance overhead, clinical-credibility gap.

Sub-30-day deployment + pre-built agents + 11 EHR integrations puts Confido squarely on the right side of that 8%. The category isn't won by the best technology. It's won by whoever closes the gap between "demo pilot" and "deployed at scale" first. The 90-day GTM plan is built around this exact insight.
