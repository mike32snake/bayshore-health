§ Five table-stakes items

What every healthcare sales playbook must have.

— 01 · Buyer-language discovery script —

Discovery questions phrased the way buyers actually talk

Generic SaaS discovery ("walk me through your current process") falls flat with a CMIO or VP Patient Access. They use specific language: abandon rate, first-call resolution, hold-time SLA, recall list, gap-in-care. Your discovery script needs to mirror their language — not yours.

Self-test: Could a new Confido AE recite the 6 questions every hospital discovery call must answer, in the buyer's exact terminology?
Our read on Confido: Site uses generic AI/automation language. Discovery script likely inherits this. Score: yellow.
— 02 · Stage definitions with exit criteria —

Every pipeline stage has a one-sentence definition + 2–4 written exit criteria

Stage definitions can't be "feels good" descriptions. They need quoted answers from the buyer. "Stage 3: Champion identified" must mean an actual named person who will lose their job if the deal fails — not a hopeful guess.

Self-test: Could a new Confido AE pass a quiz on what's required to move a deal from Stage 2 to Stage 3?
Our read on Confido: Two parallel motions (enterprise + SMB) require two stage models. Most Series A startups have one. Score: red — likely missing.
— 03 · Modular proof deck —

18–22 slides, modular, deliverable by AE as well as founder

Universal opening (3) + 4-buyer-type modular middle (12) + universal close (3). Built so swapping CMIO slides for CFO slides doesn't require rewriting the deck. The first AE you hire should be founder-equivalent on the deck within 60 days.

Self-test: How many AE-led demos went exactly the way a founder-led demo would have, last quarter?
Our read on Confido: Customer wall has 4 buyer subtypes. A non-modular deck means the AE pitches all four the same way (= bad). Score: yellow.
— 04 · Live battlecards per competitor —

Updated quarterly, not annually. Top 5 competitors only.

Each card: how they pitch (verbatim from their site) · their structural weakness (the thing they can't fix without rebuilding) · our 1-sentence answer · the customer story that nails it. Top 5 only — anything more and AEs stop using them.

Self-test: When was the last time you updated a battlecard? When was the last time an AE actually used one in a deal?
Our read on Confido: Cluster B is crowded — Hyro, Notable, Infinitus, Assort, Hello Patient. Likely no battlecards yet because category is too new internally. Score: red.
— 05 · Objection matrix —

15 most-common objections with a 1-paragraph answer + the customer story behind each

Categorized by: cost · risk · integration · change management · security/compliance · internal politics. Sourced from win/loss interviews — what the AE says, plus what the customer says back when it works.

Self-test: When an AE hears "we tried IVR and patients hated it," do they improvise — or do they recite the matrix?
Our read on Confido: Voice AI faces a specific objection set ("not another IVR") that requires its own matrix. Score: probably yellow.
§ Three things most healthtechs miss

Where Series B is won or lost.

— 06 · The thing most miss —

Buying-committee navigation, not just buyer personas

Most playbooks profile personas (CMIO, CFO, VP PA) but skip the dynamics: who blocks who, who needs to see the ROI before they'll engage, who reads which section of the proposal. A CMIO won't sign a contract — they champion. The CFO signs. The CNO can kill it. Without committee-dynamics maps, AEs sell to the most engaged person and lose to the most powerful one.

Self-test: For the last 5 stalled enterprise deals — could you name the person who blocked it, and what they needed that they didn't get?
Our read on Confido: Across 4 buyer subtypes, you'd need 4 different committee maps. Almost no Series A startups have this. Score: red — high leverage to fix.
— 07 · The thing most miss —

A "no-decision" recovery path

~30–40% of healthcare deals don't close because of competitor loss — they die because the buyer doesn't decide. Most playbooks have no plan for re-engaging or burying these deals. The "polite kill" template is as important as the close template.

Self-test: What % of last quarter's pipeline died of "no decision" vs. competitor loss? If you can't answer, your CRM hygiene is the actual problem.
Our read on Confido: Long enterprise sales cycles + no playbook for stall = pipeline accumulates "stuck" deals that look healthy but won't close. Score: probably red.
— 08 · The thing most miss —

The pilot-to-enterprise expansion narrative

Healthcare buyers don't fall in love with a pilot, sign an enterprise deal, and move on — they get stuck. Most playbooks have no explicit "if you're hearing X in month 2 of pilot, your enterprise pitch should emphasize Y" guidance. The vendor that doesn't engineer the pilot-to-enterprise transition loses the second-year ARR.

Self-test: What % of paid pilots converted to enterprise contracts in the last 12 months? What's the explicit milestone that triggers the conversation?
Our read on Confido: Sub-30-day deployment is a strength, but what happens at month 4? No public answer to this. Score: red — blind spot.
