§ 10 pricing antipatterns — scored against Confido

Five we think you're sitting on. Three we think you've avoided.

The 10 most common pricing antipatterns we see in healthtech, scored against what's publicly observable about Confido's positioning, customer mix, and category. Each antipattern is rated by how confident we are Confido is exhibiting it. The ones we'd fix first are red.

— 01 —
Likely

One price for every buyer

Healthcare buyers have radically different willingness to pay. An IDN, a specialty group, an FQHC, and a dental practice see Confido differently and have wildly different ACV ceilings. One price = either underpricing the high-WTP segment or pricing out the low one.

→ Why we think this: 4 buyer subtypes in your customer wall. No public-facing pricing tiers. AEs likely improvising on each call.
— 02 —
Likely

Per-seat pricing for non-seat value

"Per user / month" is the SaaS default but it's the wrong unit for voice AI. Healthcare buyers measure value in calls handled, FTEs replaced, appointments recovered, PMPM — not seats. Talkdesk Healthcare ($225/user/month) is the cautionary tale here; their per-seat model maps badly to actual value delivered.

→ Why we think this: Common Series A AI healthtech default. No public pricing page disagrees with this guess.
— 03 —
Likely

No discount policy — every deal a negotiation

AEs improvise. Founder approves on instinct. Each deal anchors the next one lower. Common pattern at $10M-raised Series A AI healthtech.

→ Why we think this: No published discount tiers. Likely no walk-away anchors. AEs probably checking with founder on every >15% discount.
— 04 —
Possible

Inflated list price that no one pays

"List is $X but everyone pays 30% off" — corrodes trust. Better: list price within 1.3x of actual close price; transparent volume / multi-year discounts.

→ Why we think possible: Without published pricing it's hard to tell, but the multi-buyer-subtype mix usually leads to wild list-vs-actual variance.
— 05 —
Likely

Pricing untethered to ROI math

If the buyer can't show their CFO why this pays back, they can't sign — regardless of how good your product is. Most Series A vendors quote a price; few co-build the math with the buyer using the buyer's inputs.

→ Why we think this: No public ROI calculator. "75% of calls handled" is your headline metric — buyer-readable but not buyer-defendable to a CFO.
— 06 —
Possible

"Pilot pricing" with no path to enterprise

Pilot economics accidentally become enterprise economics. Buyer expects pilot-price forever. Better: pilot pricing has explicit step-function to enterprise on stated milestones, written into pilot proposal.

→ Why we think possible: Sub-30-day deployment is a strength but creates risk: if pilot is too cheap, enterprise conversion math breaks at month 6.
— 07 —
Unlikely

No annual commitment, all month-to-month

Predictable in flat market, deadly in healthcare with 6-month sales cycles + 12-month integration paths. Better: annual minimums on enterprise; month-to-month allowed only for pilot.

→ Why we think unlikely: Series A health systems sales typically force annual contracts; your enterprise customers (York Hospital) almost certainly already on annual.
— 08 —
Possible

Add-ons that should be core (or vice versa)

Charging extra for security review or integration support that every buyer requires. Or bundling premium features into base price so no one upgrades. Better: core SKU = minimum-viable buyer-readable product. Add-ons = optional, high-margin, bought after year 1.

→ Why we think possible: 3-agent product (Sara/Ryan/Lilly) is hard to package without a clear "what's in entry vs. premium" structure.
— 09 —
Likely

Pricing that doesn't match the proof

Charge enterprise prices but don't have an enterprise reference customer. Or charge pilot prices when buyer expects 5-vendor RFP comp. Better: pricing tier matches strongest proof point you can show in the room.

→ Why we think this: Your reference list is incredibly diverse (hospital + specialty + FQHC + dental) — but each tier likely lacks 3+ similar references. CMIO at IDN #2 will ask "show me another IDN" — you have York. Owner-dentist will ask "show me another small dental" — you have Alpha.
— 10 —
Unlikely

Founder-pricing

Founder sets price by gut. AEs can't defend it. Comp set undocumented. Better: 1-page comp-set anchor with 3–5 analogous vendors, why each isn't a perfect comp, where you sit in the range.

→ Why we think unlikely: $10M Series A required investor diligence on pricing model + comp set — you've done at least the surface-level work here.
